In a previous role as an operations manager, one of my main responsibilities included ensuring that quality school schedules were sent on time for teacher and student consumption (see an example here of one of the dashboards I designed used during a school office tour).

KPI Tracked: Failure rate of jobs that ran in operational workflows

Why I Tracked This KPI: The number of failed jobs in day directly translated to the probability of ensuring schedules were sent out on time. There was also a correlation with the probability of ensuring schedule quality.

Results: After building this tracker, I was able to proactively predict spikes in failures (based on historical data) and was more aware of an increasing trend of failures for particular jobs. This was extremely helpful in identifying high priority product fixes for technology/engineering teams.

My Process

The organization has service level agreements (SLAs) that outlines program expectations for schools. The internal operations team works to ensure those SLAs are met everyday. The product and technology teams should support process improvement efforts that help to ensure our organization is adhering to our promises to our customers (aka schools).

Since I recently transitioned from the internal operations team I deeply understood the process steps and the user needs. However, I still had requirement elicitation meetings with my former team members to ensure my understanding still matched their needs.

After holding meetings with internal operations and receiving feedback from my product team on my potential approaches, I decided on a two prong approach. I began to immediately develop the user stories for the short term solution as the internal operations team communicated the increased need for a solution. I also wrote a ticket to be added to the backlog for a more robust solution that would further reduce error and increase their user experience.

Here are examples of both the low-fidelity mockup of the short-term solution that also includes the backend data flow and an example of a wireframe of the long term solution to illustrate my thoughts around how a more robust tool could handle their needs.

Result: Upon release, the internal operations team was now able to make all the necessary related changes without maintaining separate documentation. The amount of steps to make temporary changes was reduced from 4 steps to 1 step.

Problem: Engineers do not have the operational process knowledge to set up a school in our program. They need the ability to quickly create fake school data to test some new workflows in production. An informed person would take at least 2 full workdays to create the data and verify that it’s accurate and there are no operational team members available to assist in these efforts.

Solution: Develop a new tool that automates the creation of a fake school with necessary related data

My Process

The organization always had a mission to address the issue of student learning loss. This long standing need has been expedited with the reality of the global pandemic’s affect on schools. To meet this need, we need to decrease our costs to be able to reach as many schools as possible. One way to achieve this cost reduction is by re-evaluating our internal processes and prioritize automation where possible

I shadowed the internal operations team(s) responsible for completing school setup processes. I used my detailed notes to create personas which included new team members, expert team members and engineers to further determine the key user journeys to ensure they were accounted for in requirements.

Considering the other projects and ongoing initiatives in the organization, I created a detailed short-term (2 month) roadmap and a proposal for the structure of a longer term (12 month) roadmap. The roadmaps helped to communicate general staffing needs and the value proposition for process automation to senior leadership

After providing very detailed product requirements to engineers, I took an hands-off approach in tool design giving them the opportunity to design as they saw fit since they were the primary persona for the MVP version of the tool. The engineers and I remained in close contact to clarify requirements and any UX components when necessary.

Result: The MVP version of the tool was demoed to both product leaders and operational team members with much success. The amount of time to create the fake school setup data was reduced from possibly 2 days to a few seconds.
